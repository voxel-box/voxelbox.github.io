CS·07

Equestrian event entry & live scoring platform

private pilot web platform automation live scoring

The problem

Horse shows still run on paper entries, hand-built ride times, and a stressed organizer with a clipboard. Riders don't know their times until the last minute, and every schedule change ripples through the whole day by phone call.

What we built

A full entry platform: online entries with waitlists, automatic ride-time generation that respects horse and rider conflicts, live scoring published as classes run, and notifications that keep riders updated without anyone touching a phone tree. An AI concierge answers entry questions around the clock.

The outcome

An organizer can run entries, scheduling, and day-of scoring from one screen. Currently in a private pilot with a launch partner ahead of public release.

CS·06

Launch OS — from first lead to launch day

in production web platform pipeline internal tool

The problem

Winning a client is only step one. Leads, proposals, build status, and launch checklists were scattered across chats, notes, and memory — and the gap between "they said yes" and "the site is live" is exactly where projects stall.

What we built

Launch OS: a lead-to-launch platform that carries every prospect through one pipeline — from the first automated lead sweep, through proposal and build, to a launch checklist that won't let a project ship half-finished. It grew out of Client Radar and absorbed it as the front of the funnel.

The outcome

One screen answers "where is every project right now." Nothing lives in someone's head, and nothing falls through the crack between sales and delivery.

live AI agents self-hosted LLMs simulation

CS·04

The fleet — infrastructure that watches itself

in production AI agents monitoring chat ops

The problem

A growing stack of hosts, tunnels, and services means outages get noticed by users before operators. Babysitting dashboards doesn't scale, and 3 a.m. incidents don't wait for business hours.

What we built

A fleet of self-hosted agents that patrol the infrastructure on timers — watchdogs checking real service responses (not just pings), a sentinel that investigates anomalies and writes up what it found, and scheduled sweeps for the routine chores. A hybrid local/cloud AI brain does the reasoning, and everything reports into Discord, where the team can talk to the fleet and issue commands.

The outcome

The infrastructure notices its own problems and often arrives with a diagnosis before a human has looked. Operations happen in chat, with a paper trail for free.

CS·03

Client Radar — lead generation on autopilot

in production automation data pipeline internal tool

The problem

Finding local businesses that need a website is hours of manual map-scrolling and note-taking — the kind of work that quietly never gets done.

What we built

An automated sweep of open map data that finds businesses with no web presence, enriches each lead with contact details, and drafts tailored outreach — with a human approving every message before anything sends.

The outcome

Prospecting went from an afternoon of manual research to a reviewed queue that's waiting each morning. It's the same pattern we build for clients: boring, valuable work done by a system instead of a person.

CS·02

The demo pipeline — a live site in minutes

live infrastructure hosting web

The problem

The best pitch for a business without a website is showing them their site — not a portfolio PDF. But standing up a hosted, shareable demo used to cost an afternoon of server work per prospect.

What we built

A publishing pipeline on our own hardware: every demo gets its own voxelbox.org subdomain with TLS through secure tunnels — no ports opened, no per-site server setup. One JSON feed drives the catalog, and new work automatically appears on the voxelbox.org homepage the moment it's published.

The outcome

Idea to live, shareable demo in minutes. It's the machinery behind the "Floating in production" wall on our homepage — and behind every mockup we hand a prospect.

CS·01

Community game servers — where it all started

live game servers self-hosted community

The problem

Free community servers usually mean laggy, abandoned worlds; well-run ones usually mean a subscription. Players kept having to choose between a stable world and a welcoming one.

What we built

A self-hosted game server platform on hardware we own — Minecraft, Palworld, Satisfactory, and more — with a management panel, a public live-status page, scheduled backups, and update notices people can actually rely on.

The outcome

The origin of everything on this page. The worlds are still online, the communities outlived week one, and the ops discipline they demanded is the same discipline that now runs client systems.
